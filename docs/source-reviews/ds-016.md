## License landscape (read this before the rights table)

DS-016 covers two overlapping but distinct sources. Their license structure must be understood before evaluating any individual right.

### OpenStreetMap (direct)

OSM data is released under the **Open Database License 1.0 (ODbL)**. ODbL is a "share-alike + attribution" copyleft license that applies *at the database layer*, not the individual-fact layer. Three concepts from the license text drive the analysis below:

- **Derivative Database** (ODbL §1.0): "a database based upon the Database, and includes any translation, adaptation, arrangement, modification, or any other alteration of the Database or of a Substantial part of the Contents." Distributing a Derivative Database publicly triggers share-alike: the distributed copy must also be ODbL. (ODbL §4.4.)
- **Produced Work** (ODbL §1.0): "a work … resulting from using the whole or a Substantial part of the Contents (via a search or other query) from this Database." A dossier PDF, a map tile, a report, or a JSON API response that *draws on* but does not *republish* the underlying data as a database is a Produced Work. Produced Works require an attribution notice (ODbL §4.3) but **do not trigger share-alike**. (ODbL §4.5.)
- **Publicly** (ODbL §1.0): "to Persons other than You or under Your control by either more than 50% ownership or by the power to direct their activities." Internal-only use (wholly within the operator's organization) is NOT public use and triggers neither share-alike nor the attribution requirement for produced works. (Confirmed by OSM Legal FAQ.)

## Key ambiguities requiring legal confirmation

Two questions are genuinely ambiguous and this review cannot resolve them without specialist legal analysis:

**A. Does the evidence ledger constitute a publicly-used Derivative Database?**

The operator stores OSM-derived road/building features in its private Postgres ledger for internal screening. Under ODbL §1.0 and the OSM Legal FAQ, private internal use does not constitute "Public Use" and does not trigger share-alike. *However*: if dossiers delivered to paying customers include enough extracted OSM geometry or structured road-attribute data to constitute delivery of a substantial extraction of a Derivative Database (rather than a Produced Work), ODbL §4.4/§4.6 share-alike and machine-readable-copy obligations could be triggered. The distinction between a "report describing road access" (Produced Work) and "a structured data product containing road attributes" (Derivative Database distribution) is fact-specific and context-dependent. **Legal confirmation is required before the connector exports structured road-attribute tables or raw geometries to customers.** Plain-language screening evidence in a PDF/HTML dossier is almost certainly a Produced Work; structured GeoJSON road exports are more likely a Derivative Database delivery.

**B. Does mixing OSM features with non-OSM features in a single table create a Collective Database or a Derivative Database?**

The OSM Legal FAQ states that a Collective Database (independent data sources assembled without substantial OSM modification) does not trigger share-alike for the non-OSM components. The OSM Community Guidelines note that datasets are treated as independent "when a particular data type within a database consists entirely of non-OSM data within a regional cut." Where OSM and non-OSM features coexist in the same evidence table but remain distinguishable by source_registry_id, this is likely a Collective Database. **Legal confirmation is recommended** if the schema is ever refactored to blend or cross-reference OSM and non-OSM attributes in a way that makes the OSM component inseparable.

## Provenance and caveats

- Source version/date available: OSM: PBF snapshot date (YYYY-MM-DD, e.g., Geofabrik daily extract); Overture: numbered release tag (e.g., 2024-07-22.0). Both must be captured per ingestion run.
- Update cadence or freshness class: dynamic; OSM data changes continuously; Overture releases quarterly/more frequently.
- Geographic coverage limits: Global; US coverage is complete for roads and buildings; OSM accuracy varies by area and community editing activity.
- Precision/scale limits: OSM road geometry is community-contributed and not survey-grade; road centerlines may not match physical access points; road classification may differ from official county/state road records; building footprints are approximate.
- Known caveats to store with evidence: **Access proxy only. Road or building presence in OSM/Overture does not constitute evidence of legal access, public road status, maintained road status, right-of-way width, easement, title, survey accuracy, buildability, or any regulatory determination. Physical road presence ≠ legal right of access.** Treat as screening signal only; cite actual parcel-specific legal-access review as required.
- Required attribution text (OSM/ODbL themes): "Data © OpenStreetMap contributors (openstreetmap.org/copyright), available under the Open Database License (opendatacommons.org/licenses/odbl/)." Add "Overture Maps Foundation (overturemaps.org)" when using Overture ODbL themes. Include dataset vintage (PBF date or Overture release tag) and retrieval date.
- Required attribution text (Overture Places / CDLA-Permissive): Per docs.overturemaps.org/attribution/ — theme-specific attribution with contributor credits.
- Required attribution text (Overture Addresses): Per docs.overturemaps.org/attribution/ — country-specific attribution required; varies by country and source.
- Fields that must not be exposed: No owner, PII, or protected-class fields present. Do not derive or impute owner information from building footprints.
- Source failure / no-data behavior: Emit SOURCE_FAILURE evidence when bulk download fails, checksum does not match, or expected theme/area is absent. Do not silently return "no roads found" as a dossier finding without noting whether OSM coverage may be incomplete for the area.
- Reviewer notes: Approved for MVP road/building access-proxy screening with restrictions. The key ongoing compliance posture is: (a) preserve ODbL attribution in all produced works, (b) never export raw OSM geometry to users without legal review, (c) keep OSM-derived evidence distinguishable from other sources in the ledger, and (d) confirm the Derivative Database vs. Produced Work question with a lawyer before enabling any structured road-attribute export to customers.
